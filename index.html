Mosk wandered through the shadowed pines. Every ripple in the water, every trembling leaf seemed to carry the threads of fate. He paused, listening to the forest's whispered wyrd.

A faint scent of spruce filled the air, carrying whispers of ancient runes. Mosk reached out, his fingers tracing the invisible threads that connected stone to stream, pine to path, past to future.

Mategwases Lār-Lēoþ

The Teaching Songs of Mategwas

SONG I: On Rising Each Day

Be Ǣlces Dæges Ārīsenne

Hear, you who would walk in wisdom's way: When first light breaks the darkness gray, Āstād mid þonce þancful-mōd, rise with heart both glad and good.

Bless the bed that held your rest, bless the dreams that made you blessed. Grēt þone grund þe þē geheold, thank the earth that kept you whole.

Before you break your morning fast, think on all that came to pass: What wisdom did the night-time bring? What songs did sleeping spirits sing?

Mategwas teaches morning-lore: "Each dawn opens wisdom's door. Sē þe æt āwæcnunge wīs biþ, he who wakes with grateful heart finds the whole day set apart."

Stretch your limbs like young trees growing, feel life's river through you flowing. Let your first words be of praise for the gift of living days.

SONG II: On Choosing Friends

Be Frēonda Cēosenne

Listen well, you heart-hungry ones: Not all who smile are friendship's sons. Frēondscipe is fǣger gimm, true friendship is a precious gem.

Choose those who tell truth with love, who point your eyes to stars above. Flēoh þā þe flǣsclic lufu seek only flesh and passing pleasure.

A friend will hold your burdens light, walk beside you through the night. Ac sē þe symle seolf-gōd sēcð, but one who only seeks self-good brings thorns where flowers should.

Mategwas sang to those who'd hear: "Trust comes slow, but grows each year. Sē þe hrædlīce heortan giefeþ oft finds sorrow for his gift."

Better one friend tried and true than a hundred false to you. Test with small things first, then great— see who stays, and who won't wait.

Share your bread before your gold, watch whose love grows weak or bold. Gōd frēond is gōda māðm, a good friend is a golden treasure.

SONG III: On Speaking Words

Be Worda Sprecenne

Guard your tongue as guards his gold the miser in his tower cold. Word sind wyrdas, wyrcað wundru, words are fates that work great wonders.

Soft speech calms the angry heart, harsh words tear good friends apart. Ǣr þū sprice, on þīnum mōde before you speak, within your mind weigh the words you hope to find.

Will they heal or will they harm? Will they calm or cause alarm? Mategwas in wisdom spoke: "Words once said cannot be broke. Lytle word mæg lange wund macian on mōde mannes." Small word can make lasting wound in a person's heart and mind.

Better silent, seeming fool, than speaking harsh and seeming cruel. Hlyst ǣrest, spæc siþþan, listen first, then speak your part.

Truth needs not a shouting voice, gentleness should be your choice. Sweet words are like honey-dew, bitter words make all things rue.

The wise speak less but mean much more, their words unlock wisdom's door.

SONG IV: On Work and Rest

Be Weorce and Reste

All things have their proper time: seasons for the earth's sweet rhyme. Tīma is tō weorce wyrcenne, time there is for working well, tīma is tō reste ricenne, time for rest the wise can tell.

Work with hands but think with heart, let wisdom guide your working part. Nē weorce for weorulde āna, do not work for world alone, ac for gōde þe God sealde, but for good that God has sown.

He who works from dawn to dark but forgets love's sacred mark gathers gold but loses soul, misses life's most precious goal.

Mategwas taught working-song: "Labor sweet but not too long. Sē þe symle swincð būtan blisse he who toils without delight loses wisdom's clearest sight."

Rest is holy, rest is good, as the deer rests in the wood. Slǣp is sāwle sōþe nēod, sleep is soul's true need indeed.

Take your ease with grateful heart, rest well-earned for next day's start. Work and rest in rhythm flow like the tides that come and go.

SONG V: On Anger and Peace

Be Yrre and Sibbe

When anger rises like a flame, breathe deep and call on wisdom's name. Yrre is fȳr þe forswelgð eall, anger is fire that swallows all.

Count to ten, or twenty more, till the storm has passed your shore. Hrædnes ricseð, hrēow cymð æfter, haste rules now, regret comes after.

Think: what feeds this angry mood? Fear? Pride? Lack of understood? Oft under yrre ōþer sar liegeþ dīop on heortan. Often under anger lies another hurt deep in the heart.

Mategwas sang soft and low: "Let the gentle answer flow. Līþe word lǣceð sāre, gentle words can heal the sore."

Peace is not for weaklings made, but for strong ones unafraid to let their anger slowly die like embers underneath the sky.

Choose your battles, choose them few, fight for what is right and true. Sibb is strengð, nāt swicdom, peace is strength, not trickery.

The angry man rules nothing well, his fury makes his wisdom dwell in exile from his heart and home— better let your anger roam away into the empty air, and find your peace in wisdom's care.

SONG VI: On Love and Desire

Be Lufe and Wilnunge

Love comes many ways to heart: some swift like the sparrow's dart, Sume lufu langsum cymð some love slowly comes to mind like the seasons, soft and kind.

Lust and love are not the same, though they bear a kindred name. Lust wolde habban and healdan, lust would have and hold control, lufu wile giefan and weaxan, love desires to give and grow.

Young hearts burn with passion's fire, every glance stirs sweet desire. Ac wīse men wītaþ wel but the wise ones know full well: true love grows as oak trees do— slowly, surely, strong and true.

Mategwas taught lovers' lore: "Give yourself, but give no more than your heart can freely spare. Keep some mystery always there."

Love shared freely doubles joy, love held tight does love destroy. Gif þū lufige, lǣt hine gān, if you love, then let them go— gāþ hē fram þē, hē nǣs þīn, if they leave, they weren't yours, cymeþ hē eft, hē biþ ǣfre. if they return, they're yours for sure.

Trust in love, but not in blindness, balance passion with sweet kindness. Hearts that join in sacred bond grow together, strong and fond.

SONG VII: On Dealing with Enemies

Be Fēondum Wrixlenne

Even good hearts make some foes, for where truth and light both goes, Þǣr sceal scēadu scūfan wiþ, there must shadow push against brightness shining heaven-sent.

Do not seek to make enemies, but when hate comes like disease to poison minds against your name, do not play the spite-filled game.

Speak no evil of your foe, though they speak of you in woe. Gōd is gōd, þēah gāl hit hate, good is good, though evil hate tries to dim its blessed state.

Mategwas in wisdom said: "Feed your foe, give them bread. Gif þū gōde dǣst for yfele, if you do good deeds for evil, þū onbærnst glēda on his hēafde." you heap coals upon their head."

This means not revenge or harm, but love's strange and healing charm: kindness where they show you spite turns their darkness into light.

Some will soften, some will not, some stay cold, some grow hot. You cannot control their choice, but you can control your voice.

Keep your heart clean, free of hate, let your enemy's own fate rest in hands much greater than yours or any other man.

SONG VIII: On Wealth and Poverty

Be Wela and Wǣdla

Gold is good when goodly used, evil when by greed abused. Feoh biþ frēond gif hit fǣr brūceþ, money is friend if fairly used, feoh biþ fēond gif hit wōh wealdeþ. money is foe if wrongly ruled.

The poor man rich in wisdom's lore is wealthy at his very core. Wīsdōm is weorþra gimmum, wisdom is worth more than gems, richer than the richest rooms.

Do not curse your humble lot, nor envy what you have not. Mētgung is māre þonne māþmas, contentment is more than treasures, bliss is better than gold measures.

Mategwas taught to rich and poor: "Share what flows to your store. Sē þe giefð, hē giet eft, he who gives shall get again, sē þe healdeþ, hē forlīesð." he who hoards shall lose in pain.

If riches come into your hand, remember earth and sea and land belong not to human hold but to him who made the gold.

Use your wealth to heal the hurt, lift the fallen from the dirt. Poverty should make you kind, wealth should make you bear in mind those who have not what you do— golden chances to be true.

Better honest poverty than rich man's dishonesty. Your true treasure lies within: love and wisdom, free from sin.

SONG IX: On Raising Children

Be Bearnum Fēdenne

Children are as morning dew: precious, fragile, fresh and new. Bearn sind gimmas, God him lǣnde children are gems God has lent to be polished, shaped, content.

Teach them first to love what's true, show them what good persons do. Wordes lǣraþ, weorces mǣraþ, words will teach, but works proclaim louder than a speaking name.

Do not break their spirits bright with anger's crushing, hurtful might. Cnapa gecynde is tō plegan, child-nature is meant for play, laughter makes them learn each day.

Set them bounds but not too tight, guide their steps toward the light. Mategwas sang parent-song: "Firm but gentle, right not wrong. Gif þū neart for hearde, nē tō hnēsce, if you're neither harsh nor soft, ac on middume mǣðe gemeted, but measured in the middle oft, þīn bearn sceal beorht weaxan." your child shall brightly grow.

Praise good deeds, correct the bad, but let them know you're always glad they are yours to love and tend— this message clearly always send.

Children learn what parents live, more than what parents give in words alone without the deed. Be the person you would seed in their hearts for years ahead, long after words are dead.

SONG X: On Growing Old

Be Ealdunge

Gray hairs are wisdom's crown of glory, wrinkled hands tell life's full story. Yldo is ār, nā ānsien-sceaþa, age is honor, not disgrace, giefe þe God gōdum giefð. gift that God to good ones gives.

Do not rage against time's flow, but like rivers learn to go with the current, deep and slow, toward the sea all waters know.

Young limbs leap, old minds see far, youth burns bright like shooting star, Yldo scīnð swā tungol stiell, age shines like the steady star that guides the ships from afar.

Mategwas sang to silver-heads: "Share the wisdom that you've bred through the seasons of your days, teach the young the ancient ways."

Do not mourn your fading strength, celebrate your wisdom's length. Sē þe lange līfde and leornede he who long has lived and learned has a treasure to be earned by none except through years.

Tell your tales to eager ears, pass along your hopes and fears. Let the young ones know the price both of folly and advice.

Age brings freedom youth can't know: freedom to let old hurts go, freedom from the need to seem other than what dreams you dream.

Rest is sweeter when well-earned, peace is precious, wisdom learned.

Hēr endiaþ þā Tīen Lār-Lēoþ Mategwases. Sē þe hīe gehȳreþ and on heortan healdeþ, his līf sceal fægerlic flōwan swā strēam tō sǣ.

Here end the Ten Teaching Songs of Mategwas. He who hears them and holds them in heart, his life shall flow fair as a stream to the sea.
